## 1. Mission Evolution

### 1.1 Stock & Stir Exists to Preserve, Organize, and Teach Cooking Knowledge

**Decision / Idea**  
The mission of Stock & Stir was refined into a durable statement:

> **To preserve, organize, and teach cooking knowledge so every household can cook with confidence.**

**Reasoning**  
During the discussion, the project moved beyond the narrower idea of “helping people decide what to cook.” The deeper problem identified was that many households have lost not only cooking confidence, but also the informal family and community teaching structures that used to pass practical kitchen knowledge from one generation to the next.

The product’s purpose is therefore not merely to generate meals or manage pantry ingredients. It is to rebuild a bridge between ingredients, methods, household constraints, and human confidence.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This mission should guide future product, content, and engineering decisions. Features that merely increase app dependency should be treated with caution. Features that increase user confidence, practical cooking literacy, household resilience, and pantry competence should be prioritized.

---

### 1.2 SNS Is a Household Resilience Company, Not Just a Meal App

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS should be framed as a **household resilience platform** rather than simply a recipe, meal-planning, or pantry app.

**Reasoning**  
The project connects many seemingly separate ideas: pantry building, budget cooking, food insecurity, chronic illness support, low-energy cooking, freeze-drying, grocery optimization, household inventory, and food preservation. These all share one strategic center: helping households feed themselves more confidently and with less waste.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This positioning gives SNS a broader and more durable identity. It supports future expansion into pantry education, preservation workflows, food budgeting, community education, and household planning without abandoning the original mission.

---

### 1.3 “What’s for Dinner?” Is the Entry Point, Not the Full Mission

**Decision / Idea**  
The familiar dinner question remains the product’s immediate user hook, but it is not the complete mission.

**Reasoning**  
“What’s for dinner?” is the daily pain point users recognize. However, the root problem is broader: lack of cooking confidence, decision fatigue, fragmented pantry knowledge, and inability to translate available food into satisfying meals.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Marketing can continue to use the dinner question because it is accessible and emotionally immediate. Internally, however, the product should be developed as a cooking knowledge and household resilience system.

## 2. Product Philosophy

### 2.1 Build Meals. Not Recipe Collections.

**Decision / Idea**  
The phrase:

> **Build Meals. Not Recipe Collections.**

was accepted as a core SNS philosophy and likely marketing slogan.

**Reasoning**  
Traditional recipe applications begin with stored finished recipes. SNS begins with the user’s situation and assembles a meal from structured cooking knowledge. The phrase captures the philosophical and architectural distinction in plain language.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This phrase should be used on the website, investor materials, pitch decks, onboarding, and product documentation. It clearly separates SNS from recipe search products.

---

### 2.2 Competitors Organize Recipes; SNS Organizes Cooking Knowledge

**Decision / Idea**  
The following statement became a central positioning line:

> **Our competitors organize recipes. Stock & Stir organizes cooking knowledge.**

**Reasoning**  
The durable value of SNS is not a large recipe library. Its competitive advantage is the structured model of relationships among ingredients, foundations, cooking methods, sauces, constraints, substitutions, preferences, and pantry systems.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This should become one of the most important external explanations of SNS. It is concise enough for investors, customers, and future team members to remember while remaining technically accurate.

---

### 2.3 The Product Should Teach While It Serves

**Decision / Idea**  
Every meal generated by SNS should be treated as a teaching opportunity.

**Reasoning**  
Users do not only need instructions; they need understanding. Instead of simply saying “cook until done,” SNS should explain what visual, textural, or aromatic cues mean. Instead of merely producing a grocery list, it should help users understand pantry leverage.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This principle should influence recipe output, meal instructions, UX copy, pantry recommendations, video content, and help materials. SNS should build user confidence over time, not just answer the current meal request.

---

### 2.4 Success Is Measured by Cooking Confidence Created

**Decision / Idea**  
A durable success metric was proposed:

> **We do not measure success by the number of recipes in our system. We measure success by the number of families who feel confident answering one simple question: “What’s for dinner?”**

**Reasoning**  
This reframes success around user empowerment rather than content volume. It also suggests a future survey or product feedback mechanism: asking users whether they feel more confident cooking with the ingredients they already have than they did before using SNS.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This should shape KPIs, customer research, testimonials, retention analysis, and product evaluation. SNS should not chase raw recipe count as a vanity metric.

## 3. Architecture Decisions

### 3.1 One Website, One Python Engine, One Cooking Knowledge Base

**Decision / Idea**  
The website should not contain or duplicate the intelligence of SNS. The durable architecture is:

```text
Website / Front End
        ↓
Python API / Stock & Stir Engine
        ↓
Cooking Knowledge Base (CKB)
```

**Reasoning**  
The website collects user choices and displays results. The Python application contains the decision logic. The CKB stores structured cooking knowledge. Keeping these separate protects the core IP, simplifies maintenance, and allows future interfaces such as mobile apps to use the same engine.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Future developers should preserve this separation. The browser must not directly query or manipulate the database. The Python API is the contract between user interfaces and the engine.

---

### 3.2 API First: The Interface Is the Order Ticket

**Decision / Idea**  
The website/API contract should be designed before the full visual website is finalized.

**Reasoning**  
The user interface needs to know what fields to collect: protein, vegetable, foundation, time, energy, exclusions, budget, equipment, household size, and similar constraints. The Python engine needs a stable request format and a stable response format.

The metaphor adopted was:

- Website = front desk / waitress / menu
- API = order ticket
- Python engine = kitchen / chef
- CKB = cooking knowledge

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This prevents website work from becoming disconnected from engine capability. It also allows future UI redesigns without rewriting the meal-generation logic.

---

### 3.3 GoDaddy Is the Front Door; Render Runs the Brain

**Decision / Idea**  
GoDaddy should be used for the public-facing website, marketing pages, content pages, videos, pricing, and general user entry. Render should host the Python backend/API and CKB access.

**Reasoning**  
GoDaddy is simple and accessible for Lynsey to edit. Render is more appropriate for deploying production Python applications. The website is not the core product; it is the front door to the product.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The team should avoid building the core SNS engine inside GoDaddy site tools. GoDaddy is for presentation and content. Render is for application logic.

---

### 3.4 Media Should Live Outside the Python Backend

**Decision / Idea**  
Videos and images should not be stored or served directly by the Python app. The backend should store or return links to media hosted on the website, YouTube, Vimeo, CDN, or similar services.

**Reasoning**  
Videos and high-resolution images are large, bandwidth-intensive, and expensive to serve from an application backend. Python should remain responsible for meal generation, CKB logic, user constraints, subscription checks, and grocery lists.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This keeps infrastructure costs low and prevents the backend from being burdened with media delivery. Educational videos can still be tightly integrated by linking them to relevant meals, techniques, or pantry lessons.

---

### 3.5 SQLite Is Acceptable Initially; PostgreSQL Can Come Later

**Decision / Idea**  
SQLite remains acceptable for the early CKB and application stage.

**Reasoning**  
SNS requests are expected to be relatively lightweight: the user submits constraints, the Python engine reads structured data, runs decision logic, and returns a meal. The system is not initially expected to perform heavy concurrent writes or media processing.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The team should not prematurely introduce unnecessary database complexity. Migration to PostgreSQL should occur when measured workload, concurrency, or operational needs justify it.

---

### 3.6 The Database Should Be Called the Cooking Knowledge Base (CKB)

**Decision / Idea**  
Investor-facing, architecture-facing, and product-facing language should use **Cooking Knowledge Base (CKB)** rather than “database” or “recipe database.”

**Reasoning**  
“Database” understates the value of the structured cooking model. “Recipe database” is inaccurate because SNS is not primarily storing recipes. CKB communicates that the system stores structured knowledge about ingredients, techniques, relationships, substitutions, foundations, pantry logic, and household constraints.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
CKB should remain official terminology. File names such as `ckb_seed_001.db` align with this decision.

---

### 3.7 Knowledge Domains Are More Investor-Friendly Than Modules

**Decision / Idea**  
The concept of modules should be explained externally as **Knowledge Domains** where appropriate.

**Reasoning**  
Engineers understand “modules,” but investors and nontechnical collaborators more easily understand domains such as Protein Knowledge, Foundation Knowledge, Sauce Knowledge, Equipment Knowledge, Pantry Knowledge, and Food Preservation Knowledge.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Technical documentation may still use modular architecture language. Investor and business documents should emphasize Knowledge Domains to make the CKB’s value clear.

---

### 3.8 Every Piece of Knowledge Should Be Stored Once

**Decision / Idea**  
A key engineering principle was formalized:

> **Every piece of knowledge should be stored exactly once.**

**Reasoning**  
If sautéed mushrooms, mushroom cream sauce, rice cooking, or a foundation method is improved, that improvement should automatically benefit every meal path that uses it. Duplication creates inconsistency, maintenance burden, and quality drift.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This principle should guide schema design, import tooling, KO design, and meal-generation logic. Avoid storing duplicate recipe instructions where a reusable knowledge object would serve better.

## 4. Business Decisions

### 4.1 The Initial Funding Need Is Modest and Milestone-Based

**Decision / Idea**  
The team initially discussed asking for $10,000 while hoping for at least $5,000, but later recognized that the immediate launch need may be closer to a short-term self-funded gap of approximately $1,000–$2,000.

**Reasoning**  
The main early expenses are concrete and limited: Lynsey’s laptop, monitor, GoDaddy, Render, Stripe setup, and small advertising tests. The business does not require trucks, inventory, buildings, large staff, or heavy infrastructure.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
SNS should remain lean and milestone-funded. Future funding requests should be tied to specific launch or growth milestones, not vague expansion.

---

### 4.2 First Goal: Paying Subscribers, Not Fundraising

**Decision / Idea**  
The immediate business objective should be acquiring paying subscribers rather than raising money for its own sake.

**Reasoning**  
Paying users validate the product far more strongly than a pitch alone. If SNS can launch with modest self-funding and begin generating revenue, the business retains control and improves its position for any future investment conversation.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The roadmap should prioritize launch readiness, Stripe integration, beta users, and conversion to paid subscriptions.

---

### 4.3 Early Vendor Stack: GoDaddy, Render, Stripe, GitHub

**Decision / Idea**  
The early operational vendor stack should be:

- GoDaddy — website/front door/domain/content
- Render — Python backend/API/hosting
- Stripe — payments/subscriptions
- GitHub — source control and development history

**Reasoning**  
This stack is simple, cost-effective, and understandable. It avoids unnecessary enterprise infrastructure while preserving a clean path to scale.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This vendor strategy should be used in the business plan and operating model. Add new vendors only when they solve a real measured need.

---

### 4.4 Render Hobby/Pro Is Enough; Scale Is Premature

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS should begin with Render Hobby during development and move to Render Pro around public launch. Render Scale is unnecessary until measured usage proves the need.

**Reasoning**  
The app is expected to be computationally light compared with video streaming or AI inference systems. Subscriber count alone should not trigger infrastructure upgrades; concurrent usage and performance metrics should.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Infrastructure spending should follow measured demand. This reinforces the “scalable by simplicity” philosophy.

---

### 4.5 Advertising Should Be Incremental and Measured

**Decision / Idea**  
Early advertising should begin with small tests rather than large unmeasured spending.

**Reasoning**  
A $50 ad test that teaches which message converts is more valuable than a $1,000 spend with no learning loop. SNS needs evidence about audience response, not vanity reach.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Marketing budgets should be tied to experiments, conversion data, subscriber acquisition cost, and retention.

---

### 4.6 Laptop Purchase Is a Productive Asset

**Decision / Idea**  
Purchasing Lynsey’s laptop and monitor is a legitimate startup investment, not merely a household expense.

**Reasoning**  
Lynsey needs a reliable workstation for website editing, Canva, images, content management, email, Stripe, GoDaddy, and marketing. The laptop directly increases productivity and launch readiness.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Business spending should be tracked as seed-capital use. The team should distinguish productive assets from discretionary spending.

## 5. User Experience Decisions

### 5.1 The Website Should Begin With the User’s Problem

**Decision / Idea**  
The homepage should not open with technical claims. It should open with the user’s emotional and practical situation:

> **Dinner starts with what you already have.**

**Reasoning**  
Users care first about their problem, not the engine. The homepage should speak to standing in the kitchen, wondering what to cook, and needing an answer that respects time, energy, budget, and available ingredients.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The website should remain user-centered and plain-spoken. Technology should support the promise, not dominate the first impression.

---

### 5.2 “No Sign-Up Required” Trial Experience Was Considered Valuable

**Decision / Idea**  
The homepage mockup included the phrase “Try it now — no sign up required,” suggesting a possible low-friction demo experience.

**Reasoning**  
Letting users experience meal generation before committing could reduce friction and communicate value immediately.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
A public demo or limited “try it now” mode should be considered for launch or beta. It must be balanced against cost, abuse prevention, and subscription conversion.

---

### 5.3 Website UX Should Show the Meal-Building Flow Visually

**Decision / Idea**  
The homepage should communicate SNS in four simple steps:

1. Tell us what you have.
2. Set your preferences.
3. Get meals made for you.
4. Cook, enjoy, and learn/save.

**Reasoning**  
Users need to understand the product quickly. This flow emphasizes simplicity and keeps the product distinct from recipe search.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This flow should inform onboarding, app navigation, demo videos, and investor presentations.

---

### 5.4 Energy, Time, Cleanup, Equipment, and Budget Are Core UX Inputs

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS should ask not only about ingredients but also about real-life constraints such as energy, time, cleanup tolerance, equipment, household size, dietary exclusions, and budget.

**Reasoning**  
Recipe apps often ignore the actual condition of the cook. SNS is designed for real households, including people who are tired, chronically ill, busy, low on money, or limited by available equipment.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
These inputs are not optional decoration. They are part of the differentiating UX and should materially affect meal generation.

---

### 5.5 SNS Should Explain Why It Selected a Meal

**Decision / Idea**  
Meal results should eventually explain why a meal was selected: ingredients used, time fit, energy fit, pantry fit, and optional substitutions.

**Reasoning**  
Explanation builds trust and teaches users how food works. It also makes the engine feel intelligent without requiring users to understand the technical architecture.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The meal-result screen should include reasoning, not just instructions.

## 7. Knowledge Base Evolution

### 7.1 First 250 Ingredients Imported

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS reached a major CKB milestone by successfully importing the first 250 unique ingredients using an import utility.

**Reasoning**  
The import tool is more significant than the raw ingredient count because it creates a repeatable ingestion process. The team is no longer manually entering isolated records; it is building a data pipeline.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The import tool becomes part of the CKB growth infrastructure. Future large ingredient waves should build upon this pipeline.

---

### 7.2 Approximately 20 Cooking Techniques Are Being Added

**Decision / Idea**  
The project is approaching a state with approximately 250 unique ingredients and 20 techniques.

**Reasoning**  
Techniques are crucial because ingredients alone do not create cooking intelligence. Techniques turn static ingredients into executable meal paths.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Techniques should be treated as reusable knowledge objects/domains, not as buried recipe text.

---

### 7.3 The Practical Ingredient Universe Is Probably 2,000–3,500 Core Ingredients

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS does not need every product sold by Walmart or every branded variation. A mature CKB may stabilize around 2,000–3,500 core ingredients.

**Reasoning**  
Retail products include many duplicates by brand, package size, and marketing variation. SNS should normalize these into core ingredient concepts. For example, multiple brands and can sizes become “canned corn” or “canned diced tomatoes” with attributes.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Future ingredient work should prioritize normalized core ingredients and attributes over product duplication. This keeps the CKB manageable and intelligent.

---

### 7.4 Product Variations Should Be Attributes, Not Separate Core Ingredients

**Decision / Idea**  
Brand, size, organic status, low sodium, fire-roasted, and similar variations should generally be attributes of a core ingredient rather than distinct ingredient entries.

**Reasoning**  
Duplicating “diced tomatoes” for every brand and can size would inflate the database without improving meal intelligence.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
This supports clean database design, better search, simpler substitutions, and easier grocery mapping.

---

### 7.5 Signature Pantry Recipes Can Become Structured CKB Inputs

**Decision / Idea**  
A first signature pantry recipe was captured: **Signature Pantry Chicken and Rice Casserole.**

**Recipe Concept**  
Pantry ingredients included uncooked rice, cream of chicken soup, cream of mushroom soup, canned green beans, canned corn, canned chicken, chicken bouillon, and shredded cheese.

**Reasoning**  
This recipe strongly reflects SNS values: shelf-stable pantry cooking, low effort, family serving size, common ingredients, and practical comfort food.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Signature recipes can serve as examples, seed content, marketing material, and future CKB loader test cases. They also demonstrate how SNS can preserve household cooking knowledge.

## 11. Lessons Learned

### 11.1 Six Months of Discussion Enabled One Week of Execution

**Decision / Idea**  
The team recognized that the rapid progress of one week was possible because six months of prior product discovery had already established many core decisions.

**Reasoning**  
The apparent suddenness of progress was not accidental. Conversations about foundations, modular meal generation, energy filters, pantry-first logic, and recipe avoidance created conceptual clarity before implementation accelerated.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Future teams should respect discovery work. Good implementation speed often comes from slow, careful thinking beforehand.

---

### 11.2 The Import Tool Was More Important Than the First 250 Ingredients

**Decision / Idea**  
The ingredient import utility is a platform milestone.

**Reasoning**  
Manual data entry does not scale. A repeatable ingestion process turns CKB expansion into a managed pipeline.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Internal tooling should be treated as strategic infrastructure, not overhead.

---

### 11.3 The Website Is Not the Product

**Decision / Idea**  
The website is the public face and user interface, but the product’s durable value is the CKB plus the Stock & Stir Engine.

**Reasoning**  
Websites can be redesigned. The cooking knowledge model and decision engine are harder to recreate.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Do not overinvest in visual polish at the expense of the engine, API, CKB quality, and user value.

---

### 11.4 SNS Is Not Capital Intensive

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS can be launched lean.

**Reasoning**  
The company does not require inventory, vehicles, real estate, or large staff. Early costs are mainly laptop, website, hosting, payment processing, and advertising tests.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
The team should preserve ownership and avoid unnecessary fundraising unless capital clearly accelerates validated growth.

---

### 11.5 IP Protection Must Be Treated as a Portfolio

**Decision / Idea**  
SNS IP includes more than code.

**Reasoning**  
The IP portfolio includes the CKB, Stock & Stir Engine, website copy, business documents, educational content, recipes, videos, logos, taglines, architecture, and development history.

**Expected Long-Term Impact**  
Future protection work should include version control, backups, LLC formation, trademark review, copyright registration where appropriate, contributor agreements, and IP assignment agreements for contractors.
